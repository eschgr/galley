## The Forgotten Art

Long ago, before the plow and the granary, there was an art so quiet and so
complete that those who practiced it never thought to write it down. With a
gesture and a held breath a person could draw a thing out of the empty air — bread
where there had been nothing, a blade, a bridge, a wall of standing light. The
world they built had no want in it, and for a while it shone.

But anything that can make can also unmake, and in time the great houses turned the
art against one another. The war that followed was never given a name, because no
one was left who wished to remember it. It burned for a generation, and then for
another, until the towers came down and the lights went out and the knowing went
out with them.

What survived crept back to the soil. The children of the survivors learned the
plow, because the plow did not ask to be remembered; it only asked to be used.
Forests walked back over the broken roads. Vines pulled the proud walls down a
stone at a time. Within ten lifetimes the cities were hills, the hills were
forests, and the art was not even a story any longer — it was not anything at all.

## The Harvest

Ortom's village sat in a fold of green hills that had been a city once, though no
one living knew it. They knew only that the soil was good, that the river ran
clean, and that every autumn, when the last of the grain was in, the whole valley
came together to be glad.

The harvest festival was the largest day of the year. There were long tables under
the old oaks, and cider, and music that began careful and grew braver as the
evening wore on. Children ran underfoot with stolen apples. Old men argued happily
about weather that had already happened and could no longer hurt anyone.

And there was the race. There was always the race.

## The Race

Above the village rose a grey rock face, steep but climbable, its handholds worn
smooth by generations of festival racers. To reach the top first was the year's
small glory, and every harvest the young men and women lined up at its foot with
their sleeves rolled and their pride up.

Ortom was the strongest among them, and everyone knew it, and so did he. When the
call came he went up the rock like water running the wrong way, hand over hand,
never pausing to choose a hold because his body chose for him. He was at the top
before the next climber was halfway, and he turned and looked down at the long slow
line of them strung out across the face, small against the grey, and he laughed.

He had time now. He sat at the edge and kept his watch over the valley — the
tables, the thin smoke of the cooking fires, the river bending gold in the late
light — and waited for the rest to catch him up.

## The Opening

It was while he was waiting that he saw it. A little to the side of the worn path,
where the rock folded back on itself, there was an opening he had never seen — and
he had grown up on this hill, had climbed this face a hundred times, knew every
crack and ledge of it the way he knew the backs of his own hands.

He frowned at that. A man does not forget a door in a wall he has known all his
life. But there it was, dark and patient, and Ortom had never once in his life been
able to leave a question alone.

So he went in. A few steps past the mouth the daylight gave up, and the walls
changed. They were not stone anymore. They were cold beneath his palm, and hard in
a way that stone is not hard, and smooth — too smooth, smooth like nothing in the
honest world, smooth like something that had been *made*.

## The Cavern

The passage ran straight and level, which no true cave ever does, and Ortom
followed it with one hand on the cold wall until it opened all at once into a great
round chamber. The ceiling arched away into the dark above him. The floor was a
single seamless sheet. And in the very center of it, alone, stood a small dark
pillar no higher than his waist — a terminal, though he had no word for such a
thing.

It was the only object in all that emptiness, and so of course he crossed to it,
and of course he laid his hand upon it.

The pillar woke. Light ran up through it like blood returning to a cold limb, and
somewhere far down beneath the floor something that had slept a very long time
began, patiently, to turn over. From the side of the pillar an arm unfolded —
jointed, certain, unhurried — and before Ortom could pull away it had closed about
his wrist.

It did not hurt him, exactly. It simply would not be argued with. It drew him down
into a seat that rose to meet him, held him still while a second arm swung across,
and pressed something small and cold and final into the flesh of his forearm. He
felt it go in. He felt it settle, as though it had always meant to be there. Then
the light drew back down into the pillar, and the dark came up over him like water
closing over a dropped stone.

## The Waking

He woke on the hillside in the last of the daylight, flat on his back in the grass
beside the worn path, the noise of the festival drifting up faint and golden from
below.

He sat up. The rock face was only the rock face. Where the opening had been there
was nothing now but stone — smooth, blank, ordinary stone, with no door in it and
no sign there had ever been one. He pressed his hand flat against it and felt only
cool rock, and for a moment he half believed he had fallen and dreamed the whole of
it.

But there was a soreness in his forearm. A small one — the kind you stop noticing
by morning. He pushed his sleeve back and found nothing there to see: no wound, no
mark, only the faint deep ache of something underneath that had not been underneath
before.

Down in the valley someone was calling his name, laughing, asking where the winner
had got to. Ortom climbed to his feet, brushed the grass from his shoulders, and
started down toward the lights and the music, rubbing his arm without quite knowing
that he did it.

And in that soreness — unseen, unsuspected, patient as the hill itself — the old
art had come back into the world.
